SOAR: Stories That Land

Spend more time on the obstacle and the actions than on the context, and your behavioral answers stop wandering.

SITUATION
The context.
One or two sentences. Project, your role, what the team was trying to do.
Common mistake: spending half the answer here.
12% of time
OBSTACLES
What made this hard.
The interesting part. What was at stake, unknown, contested. Constraints, conflict.
Common mistake: skipping this entirely and rushing to action.
25% of time
ACTIONS
What you did.
Use "I," not "we." Decisions, conversations, tradeoffs. Specifics.
The largest budget. This is what the interviewer evaluates.
50% of time
RESULTS
What happened.
Quantify when possible. Business impact, technical outcome, customer effect.
Common mistake: burying the outcome under more context.
13% of time

RESOURCE 10 · BEHAVIORAL · EXAMPLES

Three SOAR stories in full

LEADING WITHOUT AUTHORITY
The API contract nobody could agree on.
S
Situation
I was a senior IC on a four-person backend team rebuilding the billing API. No tech lead on the team.
O
Obstacles
Our manager left mid-quarter. Three engineers, three different proposals for the contract. PM was waiting on a decision to plan downstream work. Six weeks until the deadline and nobody had authority to call it.
A
Actions
I set up a daily 20-minute sync. Built a decision matrix with five axes: latency, schema flexibility, migration cost, blast radius, and team familiarity. Walked through it in a doc, named my pick, and asked for written disagreement by Friday. When two engineers pushed back, I rewrote the matrix with their inputs and re-shared. I also volunteered to write the integration tests so they could keep moving.
R
Results
Shipped on time. The contract has held through three follow-on features. I was promoted that cycle, and the matrix template is still in our team doc.
FAILING AND LEARNING
The migration that almost cost us $30K a day.
S
Situation
I led the migration of our payment service to a new processor.
O
Obstacles
I underestimated the reconciliation work. In week one of the rollout, finance flagged a daily delta of around $30K that we couldn't explain. The CFO wanted to roll back; I had two days to prove we shouldn't.
A
Actions
I paused new traffic at 10%. Ran a three-day audit with one engineer and one finance analyst. Found a missing field in the mapping for partial refunds. Wrote a backfill script, paired with finance to verify it against three months of history, and built a pre-flight check that compares end-to-end ledgers before any future cutover.
R
Results
Caught the bug before full rollout. Wrote the postmortem; the pre-flight check is now standard on every migration my team runs.
PUSHING BACK ON A DECISION
When the VP wanted to skip the load test.
S
Situation
We were two weeks from launching a new onboarding flow expected to triple sign-up traffic.
O
Obstacles
The VP wanted to cut the load test to hit the date. I disagreed, but I was the most junior engineer in the room and our staff engineer had stayed quiet.
A
Actions
I put the risk in dollars: $40K per hour of downtime, 35% chance of a capacity issue. Proposed a four-hour pared-down test instead of the two-day one. Sent it in writing, copied the staff engineer.
R
Results
Surfaced two queue-depth issues we'd have hit in production. Launch was clean. Load tests are now standard.
